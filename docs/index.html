Question 1a

Using the NYS DOL's employment statistics data for the latest available month, discuss which major industries (by 2-digit NAICS) in New York City changed the most over the prior year. Describe possible reasons why these industries experienced greater change than other industries.

The latest available month is February 2026. NYC total nonfarm employment stood at 4,791,000, down 39,400 (−0.82%) from February 2025. Twelve of eighteen sectors contracted; only six grew.

Year-over-Year Employment Change by Industry (Feb 2026 vs Feb 2025)

Top declining industries:

  • Manufacturing (NAICS 31-33): −5.82% (−3,100 jobs) — continued long-term secular decline driven by offshoring and automation.
  • Arts, Entertainment & Recreation (NAICS 71): −5.34% (−4,700 jobs) — after a strong post-COVID recovery, employment is normalizing. Consumer spending patterns are shifting amid broader economic softening.
  • Mining & Construction (NAICS 21+23): −4.82% (−6,600 jobs) — elevated interest rates throughout 2024–2025 dampened new development. NYC housing construction faces headwinds despite high demand.
  • Health Care & Social Assistance (NAICS 62): −2.32% (−24,200 jobs) — the largest absolute decline, but primarily driven by a policy change (CDPAP reform, April 2025), not economic contraction. The state consolidated home health care fiscal intermediaries, causing a reclassification of roughly 79,000 workers.
  • Accommodation & Food Services (NAICS 72): −1.51% (−5,300 jobs) — the sector recovered strongly from COVID but is now experiencing saturation and margin pressure from rising wages and reduced downtown foot traffic.

Top growing industries:

  • Information (NAICS 51): +1.94% (+4,200 jobs) — growth in streaming services and digital content production.
  • Finance & Insurance (NAICS 52): +1.61% (+6,100 jobs) — steady growth consistent with NYC's role as a global financial center.
  • Government (NAICS 92): +1.58% (+9,500 jobs) — continued restoration of public sector staffing following COVID-era attrition.

According to the NYC Comptroller's February 2026 report, there was essentially no net job creation outside health care during 2025, and the economy is characterized as a "low hire, low fire" environment.

Question 1b

How was the change that these industries experienced over the last year different from what they experienced over the last five years? Please include all analysis (calculations, tables, charts, etc.) in your response.

The 1-year and 5-year comparisons tell very different stories because the 5-year baseline (February 2021) was still deep in COVID disruption. Large positive 5-year figures reflect recovery from pandemic lows, not sustainable organic growth.

1-Year vs 5-Year Employment % Change: Top Movers

Key contrasts:

  • Accommodation & Food Services: 1-year = −1.51%, but 5-year = +87.0% (from 185,100 to 346,200). The sector nearly doubled from its COVID low, but has now begun contracting — the recovery is complete and the sector is plateauing.
  • Arts, Entertainment & Recreation: 1-year = −5.34%, but 5-year = +77.2% (from 47,000 to 83,300). Massive post-COVID recovery, now normalizing.
  • Health Care & Social Assistance: 1-year = −2.32%, but 5-year = +29.9% (+234,500 jobs). The 5-year gain is largely real growth (aging population, expanded home health programs). The 1-year decline is a CDPAP policy artifact.
  • Manufacturing: 1-year = −5.82%, 5-year = −3.65%. Declining in both periods — a decades-long secular trend, not a COVID effect.
  • Information: 1-year = +1.94%, 5-year = +5.23%. One of the few sectors growing in both periods, suggesting sustained structural growth.

Question 2a

Using the API docs, pull and export only the following data. Please include the exact query that you used (in the form of https://...) in your response.

The query filters for all 311 service requests where:

  • created_date is between 12/15/2021 and 3/15/2022 (inclusive)
  • agency_name is NYPD or HPD
  • complaint_type starts with "Noise" (captures all subcategories) or equals "Illegal Parking"

Exact query URL:

https://data.cityofnewyork.us/resource/erm2-nwe9.json?$select=unique_key,created_date,agency_name,complaint_type,descriptor,borough,incident_zip,latitude,longitude&$where=created_date between '2021-12-15T00:00:00' and '2022-03-15T23:59:59' AND (agency_name='New York City Police Department' OR agency_name='Department of Housing Preservation and Development') AND (starts_with(complaint_type, 'Noise') OR complaint_type='Illegal Parking')&$limit=500000

This returned 198,158 rows, all from NYPD. HPD returned zero matching rows because HPD handles housing quality (heat/hot water, plumbing) — not noise or parking complaints.

Note on noise matching: The 311 system categorizes noise into subcategories ("Noise - Residential", "Noise - Commercial", etc.). An exact match for complaint_type = 'Noise' would capture only the ~10,800 records handled by the Department of Environmental Protection — missing 92% of noise complaints. Using starts_with(complaint_type, 'Noise') captures the full scope as the question intends. The exact "Noise" type belongs to DEP (not NYPD or HPD), so it is correctly excluded by the agency filter.

Question 2c

Describe one way to visualize this data and include the visualization in your response. What do you want policymakers to know about your findings?

Visualization: A horizontal bar chart showing complaint volume by type, color-coded by category (red for noise subcategories, blue for illegal parking). This allows immediate visual comparison of the scale and composition of complaints. A supplementary weekly time series shows seasonal patterns over the 3-month window.

Weekly Complaint Trends: Noise vs Illegal Parking

What policymakers should know:

  • Residential noise dominates. With 75,543 complaints (38% of all complaints), residential noise is the single largest quality-of-life concern reported to 311. Dense living conditions in multi-unit buildings are a persistent source of friction.
  • Illegal parking rivals all noise combined. At 86,493 complaints (44%), illegal parking is nearly as large as the entire noise category (111,665). This represents a significant enforcement burden on NYPD and raises the question of whether parking enforcement could be handled by a civilian agency.
  • Quality-of-life enforcement consumes substantial NYPD resources. Nearly 200,000 complaints over three months is an extraordinary volume. If the city wants to rethink policing priorities, understanding the volume and nature of 311 complaints is an essential starting point.

Question 2d

Ask one research question about this data; discuss additional data that you would merge with this dataset to answer your question and write a simple equation that shows the relationship. Why is studying that relationship important for policymaking?

Research question: Do neighborhoods with lower median household incomes generate more noise and illegal parking complaints per capita, after controlling for population density?

Additional data needed: American Community Survey (ACS) 5-Year Estimates from the U.S. Census Bureau — specifically median household income, total population, and land area by zip code tabulation area (ZCTA). The 311 data contains incident_zip, which can be merged with ACS ZCTA-level data.

Equation (OLS regression):

ComplaintsPerCapitai = β0 + β1 · MedianIncomei + β2 · PopulationDensityi + εi

Where β1 is the key coefficient of interest: if negative and statistically significant, it would indicate that lower-income neighborhoods file more complaints per person, after accounting for population density.

Why this matters for policymaking:

  • Resource allocation equity: If lower-income neighborhoods generate more complaints per capita, it could indicate greater quality-of-life challenges (noise from overcrowded housing, inadequate parking infrastructure). City services should be directed proportionally to where need is greatest.
  • Complaint-driven vs. need-driven governance: 311 data reflects who files complaints, not necessarily who experiences the most problems. Higher-income residents may be more likely to file complaints, biasing resource allocation toward wealthier neighborhoods. Understanding this relationship helps distinguish between actual need and reporting behavior.
